  # Sunshine Coast Businesses: The FortiBleed Attack Shows Why Your Login Credentials Are Gold to Cybercriminals

If you're running a small business on the Sunshine Coast—whether you're a tradie, accommodation host, or salon owner—you might think hackers only target big corporations. The reality is quite different. A massive cyber attack called "FortiBleed" has just been exposed, and it reveals exactly how criminals are stealing business login details to break into systems weeks or even months later.

Here's what you need to know, and why it matters to your business.

## What Actually Happened with FortiBleed?

Security researchers have uncovered a large-scale credential theft campaign that targeted businesses using Fortinet security equipment. The attackers weren't after money immediately—they were collecting usernames and passwords like someone casing houses before a robbery.

The stolen credentials have now been linked to two ransomware groups called INC and Lynx. These groups use stolen logins to quietly break into business networks, look around, and then lock everything up and demand payment.

Think of it this way: someone photocopied your office keys months ago, and they've just decided to use them.

## Why Small Businesses Are in the Crosshairs

You might be thinking, "I don't use Fortinet equipment—I barely know what that is." Fair enough. But this attack illustrates a much bigger problem affecting SME cyber security across Australia.

**Here's what makes small businesses vulnerable:**

* You're using the same password across multiple platforms—your MYOB account, Xero, Square, Gmail, and Instagram
* You've never changed the login details since your accountant or web designer set everything up
* You don't have someone monitoring for suspicious login attempts
* You assume that if something's wrong, someone will tell you

Cybercriminals know this. They specifically target small business cyber security weaknesses because there's usually no IT team watching the gate.

## A Real-World Scenario for Sunshine Coast Trades

Imagine you're an electrician on the Sunshine Coast. You run jobs through ServiceM8, invoice through Xero, and keep client details in Google Sheets. Someone gets hold of your login credentials—maybe through a data breach you never heard about, or a fake email you clicked last year.

Three months later, they log in. They look legitimate because they're using *your* credentials. They download your customer list. They lock you out of Xero right before end-of-month invoicing. Then they demand $5,000 in Bitcoin to give you back access.

Meanwhile, you've got customers waiting for invoices, suppliers to pay, and no way to prove what you're owed.

This isn't scaremongering—this is the business model behind campaigns like FortiBleed.

## What This Means for Cyber Security on the Sunshine Coast

Whether you're running a café in Noosa, an Airbnb in Maroochydore, or a landscaping business in Caloundra, your login credentials are valuable. Not just to you—to criminals planning their next ransomware attack.

The challenge with SME data breaches is that you often don't know you've been compromised until it's too late. Stolen credentials can sit unused for months while attackers plan their move or sell them to someone else.

**What you can do right now:**

* Use different passwords for different accounts
* Turn on two-factor authentication wherever possible
* Be suspicious of unexpected login alerts or password reset emails
* Consider whether your business logins have already been exposed in a previous breach

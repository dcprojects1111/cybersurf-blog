# The Check Point VPN Flaw: What Sunshine Coast Business Owners Need to Know

You might have seen headlines recently about a security flaw in Check Point VPN systems being exploited by cybercriminals. It sounds technical, and frankly, a bit scary. But before you panic, let's break down what actually happened, whether it affects your business, and what you should do about it.

## What Happened?

Israeli cybersecurity company Check Point discovered a critical security flaw in their Remote Access VPN and Mobile Access systems. Think of a VPN (Virtual Private Network) as a secure tunnel that lets your staff connect to your business systems when they're working from home or on the road. It's the digital equivalent of giving them a secure key to your office, rather than leaving the door wide open to the street.

The problem? Criminals found a weak spot in Check Point's tunnel before the company knew it existed. They exploited this vulnerability in what's called a "zero-day attack" — basically, they had a head start before anyone could fix it. These attackers have been linked to the Qilin ransomware gang, a particularly nasty crew who break into business networks, lock up all the files, and demand payment to give them back.

Check Point has now released security updates to patch the flaw. If your business uses their VPN systems, those updates need to be applied immediately.

## Does This Affect Your Business?

Here's the honest answer: **only if you're using Check Point's specific VPN products**.

Most small businesses on the Sunshine Coast aren't running Check Point systems — they're enterprise-grade tools typically used by larger organisations or businesses with dedicated IT departments. If you're a café owner, accountant, or tradie with a handful of staff, chances are you're either not using a VPN at all, or you're using something else entirely.

**How to know for sure:**

- Ask whoever manages your IT: "Do we use Check Point VPN or Mobile Access?"
- Check any documentation from when your remote access was set up
- If you're genuinely not sure and staff work remotely, it's worth a quick call to your IT provider

If the answer is yes, contact your IT support immediately and ensure the security patch has been applied. This isn't something to put off — it's already been exploited in the wild.

### What If I'm Not Affected?

Even if you're not using Check Point, this incident is a useful reminder about two bigger cyber security issues that affect every small business.

## The Real Takeaway: Remote Access Is a Target

Whether you're in Noosa or Nambour, if your team can access business systems remotely, you need to know those connections are secure. Remote access — whether it's a VPN, cloud accounting software, or just logging into your server from home — is one of the most common entry points for an SME data breach.

**Here's what you can do this week:**

- **Ask the access question**: Who in your business can log in remotely? Make a list. If someone left six months ago and still has access, that's a problem.
- **Turn on multi-factor authentication (MFA)**: This is the digital equivalent of needing both a key and a code to open your door. It stops most break-ins before they start. Most systems — MYOB, Xero, Microsoft 365 — offer this for free.
- **Schedule an IT security audit**: Even a basic review can spot obvious gaps before criminals do.

You don't need to become a cybersecurity expert. You just need to know what's unlocked and who has the keys.

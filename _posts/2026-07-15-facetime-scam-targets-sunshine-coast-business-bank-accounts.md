  # Scammers Are Using FaceTime to Target Bank Accounts: What Sunshine Coast Business Owners Need to Know

If you run a small business on the Sunshine Coast and use your phone for everything from taking bookings to managing your accounts, there's a new scam you need to be aware of. Cybercriminals are now using FaceTime and similar video calling apps to trick business owners into handing over their banking details—and it's working.

## How the FaceTime Scam Works

This isn't your typical phishing email. Scammers are getting more sophisticated, and they're targeting the tools you use every day.

Here's what's happening:

* A business owner receives a FaceTime call that appears to be from their bank, payment platform, or even a trusted supplier
* The caller looks professional, sounds convincing, and creates a sense of urgency—perhaps claiming there's a suspicious transaction that needs immediate verification
* They ask you to confirm details, reset passwords, or approve transactions while you're on the call
* Meanwhile, they're using unpatched security holes in your device to access your actual banking apps or payment systems in the background

For a café owner on Mooloolaba Esplanade managing Square payments between the morning rush, or an electrician in Nambour checking their bank balance after a job, these calls can feel legitimate—especially when you're busy and distracted.

## Why Small Businesses Are Being Targeted

If you've ever thought "I'm too small for hackers to bother with," it's time to reconsider. Small business cyber security isn't just for tech companies anymore.

Sole traders and micro-businesses are actually prime targets because:

* You're managing business banking from your personal phone
* You don't have IT support watching your back
* You're busy running your business and more likely to let your guard down
* One successful scam can net criminals thousands of dollars with relatively little effort

An SME data breach doesn't just mean losing money from your account. It can mean:

* Losing access to your payment systems during your busiest period
* Having customer payment details compromised
* Damaging the reputation you've built in the local community
* Spending days or weeks trying to recover accounts and funds

## The "Unpatched Device" Problem

One reason these scams are working is that criminals are exploiting security vulnerabilities in phones and tablets that haven't been updated.

Think of software updates like getting your electrical work checked—they're not just adding new features, they're fixing dangerous problems that could cause serious damage.

When you ignore that "Software Update Available" notification:

* Known security holes remain open
* Scammers can exploit these weaknesses to access your device remotely
* Your banking apps, booking systems, and business accounts become vulnerable

This is a critical part of small business cyber security that most Sunshine Coast operators simply aren't aware of.

## Protecting Your Business: Practical Steps

You don't need to be a tech expert to protect yourself. Here's what you should do today:

**Update everything**
* Install all pending updates on your phone, tablet, and computer
* Turn on automatic updates if possible
* Don't postpone—do it tonight when you're closed

**Be suspicious of unexpected video calls**
* Your bank will never call you on FaceTime to verify account details
* If someone claims there's an urgent banking issue, hang up and call your bank directly using the number on their official website
* Never share passwords, codes, or approve transactions during an unexpected call

**Separate business and personal where possible**
* Consider using different devices or at least different accounts for business banking
* Use strong, unique passwords for banking and payment apps
* Enable two-factor authentication on every account that offers it

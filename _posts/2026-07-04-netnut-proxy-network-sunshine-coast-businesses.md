  # Massive Proxy Network Disrupted: What Sunshine Coast Business Owners Need to Know

If you run your business from your phone — managing bookings, sending quotes, or processing payments — there's something you need to know about a cyber threat that was just shut down this week.

Google, working with cybersecurity experts, has just disrupted a massive criminal network called NetNut. This network had quietly infected more than 2 million devices worldwide, including smartphones, smart TVs, and streaming boxes. Many of these devices belonged to everyday people and small business owners who had no idea their equipment had been compromised.

## What Actually Happened?

NetNut operated what's called a "residential proxy network." Think of it like criminals renting out secret tunnels through other people's devices. Here's how it worked:

* Malicious software infected millions of Android devices, often through dodgy apps
* Criminals then used these infected devices to hide their online activities
* This let them bypass security systems, scrape data, and carry out attacks while appearing to come from legitimate devices
* The device owners — possibly including Sunshine Coast small business owners — had no idea it was happening

The scary part? Your phone or tablet could be working perfectly fine while still being part of a criminal network in the background.

## Why This Matters for Your Business

Imagine you're a landscape gardener on the Sunshine Coast. You've got your smartphone with your invoicing app, your banking app, and all your client contacts. If your phone gets infected with this type of malware, criminals aren't just using your device as a tunnel — they potentially have access to everything on it.

That means:

* Your client database and contact information
* Access to business banking and payment apps
* Your email accounts and correspondence
* Login credentials for job management or booking systems
* Sensitive information about your projects and quotes

For an SME data breach, the damage goes beyond just the immediate hack. You could face loss of client trust, regulatory issues if customer data is exposed, and significant downtime while you sort everything out.

## How Does Your Device Get Infected?

Most business owners don't deliberately download malicious software. These infections typically happen through:

* Downloading apps from unofficial sources or third-party app stores
* Clicking links in suspicious emails or text messages
* Installing "free" versions of paid apps
* Connecting to unsecured public Wi-Fi networks
* Not keeping your device software updated

## What You Should Do Right Now

Small business cyber security doesn't have to be complicated or expensive. Here are practical steps you can take today:

**Audit Your Devices**
* Check what apps are installed on your work phone or tablet
* Remove anything you don't recognise or haven't used in months
* Only download apps from official stores (Google Play, Apple App Store)

**Update Everything**
* Enable automatic updates for your device operating system
* Keep all your business apps updated
* Don't ignore those update notifications

**Practice Basic Cyber Hygiene**
* Use different passwords for different accounts
* Enable two-factor authentication on business apps
* Be suspicious of unexpected links, even from known contacts
* Avoid using public Wi-Fi for business activities

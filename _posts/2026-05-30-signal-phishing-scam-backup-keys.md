# Signal Users Being Targeted in Clever Phishing Scam

If you or your team use Signal to communicate—maybe for confidential client conversations or sensitive business chats—there's a sneaky scam doing the rounds you should know about.

Cybercriminals are pretending to be Signal's support team and tricking people into handing over their backup recovery keys. Think of these keys like the master code to a safe: once the crooks have it, they can unlock your entire message history. Every conversation. Every photo. Every detail you thought was private.

Here's the thing: Signal is known for being secure. That's why people use it. But no app can protect you from willingly handing the keys to the wrong person—and that's exactly what this scam relies on.

## How the Scam Works

You get a message, often an email or even a direct message within Signal itself, claiming to be from Signal Support. It might say there's been a security issue with your account, or that you need to "verify your backup" to avoid losing your messages.

The message looks professional. It might use Signal's logo, the right colours, even sound official. It then asks you to share your backup recovery key—usually a long string of numbers and letters—so they can "restore" or "secure" your account.

Once you share it, they've got full access to your message archive. Everything you've ever sent or received.

It's the digital equivalent of someone in a hi-vis vest knocking on your door, claiming to be from the council, and asking for your house keys to "check the plumbing." You wouldn't hand them over—but in the moment, especially if you're busy, it's easier to be caught off guard.

## Does This Affect Sunshine Coast Small Businesses?

Honestly? Only if you or your team use Signal.

If you're running a plumbing business, a bookkeeping practice, or a physio clinic on the Sunshine Coast and Signal isn't part of your daily toolkit, this particular scam isn't going to hit you directly. But the *method* behind it absolutely should be on your radar, because it's the same trick used in countless other phishing attacks—just with a different costume.

What makes this one worth paying attention to is how targeted it is. The scammers aren't blasting out generic "click here" emails. They're going after people who use a specific app, with a specific goal: stealing something valuable that unlocks everything else.

That's the playbook now. And it's being used everywhere—from fake Xero login pages to dodgy MYOB emails.

### What You Can Actually Do About It

Even if Signal's not your thing, here are two simple things you can action this week:

- **Teach your team this rule:** No legitimate support team will ever ask you for passwords, recovery codes, or backup keys. Not Signal. Not Microsoft. Not your bank. If someone asks, it's a scam. Full stop.

- **Set a company policy:** Any message claiming to be from a tech provider asking for sensitive info gets forwarded to whoever handles your IT (even if that's just your most tech-savvy staff member) before anyone responds. A two-minute pause can save you months of cleanup after an SME data breach.

## The Bottom Line

Cybercriminals are getting smarter. They're not just spraying and praying anymore—they're doing their homework and crafting messages that look and feel real.

You don't need to become a cyber security expert to protect your business. You just need to slow down, ask questions, and never hand over the keys—digital or otherwise—without double-checking who's really on the other side of the door.

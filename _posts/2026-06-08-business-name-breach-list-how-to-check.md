# Is Your Business Name on the Latest Breach List? Here's How to Check Without the Panic

You've probably seen the headlines: another data breach, millions of records exposed, chaos everywhere. But here's what those breathless news alerts rarely tell you — whether *your* small business is actually affected, and what you should do about it if you are.

Let's cut through the noise. Data breaches happen constantly, but not every breach matters to every business. What does matter is knowing how to quickly work out if you're caught up in one, and what simple steps to take next. No IT degree required.

## What Actually Happens in a Data Breach?

Think of a data breach like someone breaking into a filing cabinet at a business you deal with — your accountant, your supplier, or the software company that handles your invoicing. They've grabbed a copy of whatever was inside: names, email addresses, sometimes passwords or payment details.

The tricky bit? You might not find out for months. The business that got broken into has to investigate, notify authorities, and then contact affected customers. By the time you hear about it, your information could already be floating around online.

For small business owners on the Sunshine Coast — whether you're running a busy café in Mooloolaba or a plumbing business covering Noosa to Caloundra — this isn't about panic. It's about knowing where you stand and taking sensible precautions.

## How to Check If Your Business Is Affected

Here's the practical bit. There are a few simple ways to find out if your business email or accounts have been part of a known breach:

**Use a breach checking service.** Websites like Have I Been Pwned let you enter your email address and instantly see if it appears in any major breaches. It's free, legitimate, and takes about 30 seconds. If you've got multiple email addresses across your business, check them all.

**Watch your inbox.** If a service you use has been breached, they're legally required to notify you. Don't ignore those emails — even if they look like marketing. Read them carefully for instructions on resetting passwords or monitoring accounts.

**Check with your IT support or security provider.** If you've got someone handling your IT security, they should be monitoring this stuff already. If they're not bringing breaches to your attention proactively, it might be time for a proper IT security audit.

### What to Do If You Find a Match

If your business email shows up on a breach list, don't spiral. Here's your action plan:

- **Change your password immediately** — on the breached service and anywhere else you've used the same password (yes, we know you shouldn't reuse them, but let's deal with reality first).
- **Turn on two-factor authentication** wherever it's available. That's the thing where you get a code sent to your phone as well as entering a password.
- **Monitor your accounts closely** for a few weeks — bank accounts, business software, anything connected to that email address.

## Two Things Every Sunshine Coast Business Owner Should Do This Week

Even if you're not affected by a current breach, here are two practical steps that take less time than your morning coffee:

**Set unique passwords for critical accounts.** Your banking, your accounting software, your payment systems — these should all have different passwords. Use a password manager if juggling them feels impossible.

**Enable alerts on your business accounts.** Most banks and software platforms will text or email you when something unusual happens. Switch these on.

Look, an SME data breach doesn't have to be catastrophic. What matters is responding quickly and sensibly when you discover your information has been exposed. No drama, just action.

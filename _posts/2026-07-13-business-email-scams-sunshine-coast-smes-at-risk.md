  # Your Business Email Could Be Sending Scams Right Now — and You'd Never Know

**A wake-up call for Sunshine Coast small business owners who think cybercrime only happens to the big end of town**

Running a small business on the Sunshine Coast means wearing a dozen hats at once. Between managing clients, chasing invoices, and keeping the day-to-day ticking over, cybersecurity probably isn't high on your to-do list. But here's something that should grab your attention: right now, cybercriminals are hijacking business email accounts to send scams — and the business owner often doesn't find out until it's too late.

## What's Actually Happening?

Email account takeovers aren't new, but they're becoming frighteningly common for small businesses. Here's how it works: a scammer gets hold of your email login details (usually through phishing, weak passwords, or previous data breaches) and quietly logs into your account. They don't lock you out or delete everything. Instead, they watch and wait.

They study how you communicate with clients and suppliers. They learn your tone, your invoicing patterns, when you're typically offline. Then, at just the right moment, they strike — sending legitimate-looking emails from your actual account asking clients to pay invoices to a different bank account, or requesting urgent payments for fake emergencies.

## Why Small Businesses Are Prime Targets

You might think, "I'm just a small operation — why would anyone bother?" That's exactly why you're attractive to cybercriminals. 

Small businesses typically have:

- Less sophisticated security measures in place
- No dedicated IT support monitoring for suspicious activity
- High levels of trust with their clients and suppliers
- Direct access to money through invoicing and payment systems

An SME data breach doesn't make headlines like a major corporation's breach would, which means criminals can operate under the radar for longer.

## A Real-World Example: The Landscaper Who Lost Everything

Imagine you're a landscaper with 30 regular clients across Noosa, Maroochydore, and Caloundra. Your email gets compromised, and the scammer sends invoices to your clients with updated banking details — their details. Three clients pay before anyone notices. By the time you discover what's happened, $8,000 is gone, your professional reputation is damaged, and you're facing uncomfortable conversations with clients about who should wear the loss.

This isn't a hypothetical scenario. It's happening to tradies, retailers, and service providers right across Australia.

## Warning Signs Your Email May Be Compromised

Keep an eye out for these red flags:

- Clients or suppliers mentioning emails you never sent
- Sent items in your mailbox you don't recognise
- Login notifications from locations you've never been
- Customers asking about payment details you didn't change
- Unusual rules or filters appearing in your email settings

## What You Can Do Today

Small business cyber security doesn't have to be complicated or expensive. Start with these basics:

**Enable two-factor authentication** on your email account — it's free and makes it much harder for criminals to access your account even if they have your password.

**Use unique, strong passwords** for different accounts. Consider a password manager if remembering them all becomes overwhelming.

**Be suspicious of unexpected emails** asking you to click links or verify account details, even if they look legitimate.

**Check your email settings regularly** for forwarding rules or unfamiliar connected devices.

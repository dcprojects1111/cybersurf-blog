# Amazon Job Scam Texts: What Sunshine Coast Small Business Owners Need to Know

If you've received a text message lately promising easy money for "reviewing products" or "optimising listings" for Amazon, you're not alone. Scammers are ramping up a sophisticated con that's catching out busy business owners across Australia — and right here on the Sunshine Coast.

## The Scam That Sounds Too Good to Be True

The text usually arrives out of the blue. It promises high-paying, flexible work with Amazon — sometimes $200-$500 a day for minimal effort. All you need to do is click a link, sign up, and start "tasks" like rating products or boosting seller listings.

Here's the problem: Amazon has nothing to do with it.

These messages are part of an elaborate scam designed to steal your money, personal information, and in some cases, take over your business accounts. For sole traders and micro-businesses operating mainly from a mobile phone, the consequences can be devastating.

## How the Scam Actually Works

The trap is set in stages:

**Stage 1: The Hook**  
You receive a text offering legitimate-sounding remote work with Amazon. The message often includes professional language and a convincing link.

**Stage 2: The Platform**  
After clicking through, you're taken to a slick-looking website or app. You might even complete a few small tasks and receive small payments to build trust.

**Stage 3: The Sting**  
Eventually, you're told you need to pay a "deposit" or "unlock" higher-paying tasks by transferring money. Alternatively, you're asked to provide banking details, driver's licence information, or business credentials "for payment purposes."

**Stage 4: The Theft**  
Once scammers have your money or information, they disappear — or worse, use your details to access other accounts, apply for credit in your business name, or compromise your payment systems.

## Why This Matters for Sunshine Coast Businesses

Imagine you're a café owner juggling morning rush, suppliers, and staff rosters. A text promising easy side income sounds tempting. You click through during a quiet moment, enter some details, and suddenly your business banking app is compromised. Your Friday takings vanish. Your suppliers aren't paid. Your reputation takes a hit.

Or perhaps you're an electrician who provided identity documents thinking you were setting up a legitimate gig. Weeks later, you discover someone has used your ABN to order equipment or worse — apply for business loans.

These aren't far-fetched scenarios. Small business cyber security breaches often start with something that seems harmless: a text, an email, a too-good offer.

## Red Flags to Watch For

Protect yourself by recognising these warning signs:

- Unsolicited job offers via SMS, especially promising high pay for low effort
- Requests to pay money upfront to "unlock" earnings or "verify" your account
- Pressure to act quickly or claims of "limited positions available"
- Links that don't go to official Amazon domains (amazon.com.au)
- Requests for banking passwords, driver's licence scans, or business login credentials
- Payment via cryptocurrency, gift cards, or untraceable methods

## What You Should Do Right Now

**Don't click links in unsolicited job offer texts.** If you're interested in Amazon work programs, go directly to Amazon's official website and navigate to their jobs section yourself.

**Enable two-factor authentication** on your business accounts — banking, invoicing software, booking systems, social media, everything. It's the single best protection against account takeovers.

**Be sceptical of too-good offers.** Legitimate remote work exists, but if someone's offering $500 a day for rating products, your alarm bells should be ringing.

**Report suspicious messages** to Scamwatch (scamwatch.gov.au) and delete them.

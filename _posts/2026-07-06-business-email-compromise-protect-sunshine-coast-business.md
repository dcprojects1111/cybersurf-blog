  # Business Email Compromise: The $300,000 Scam Targeting Sunshine Coast Small Businesses

If you're running a small business on the Sunshine Coast and someone sends you an email that looks like it's from your accountant, your supplier, or even a tradie you've worked with before — how do you know it's really them?

Business Email Compromise (BEC) is one of the fastest-growing cyber threats in Australia right now, and it's not just happening to big corporations. It's hitting sole traders, micro-businesses, and local operators who handle their entire business from their phone or laptop.

## What Is Business Email Compromise?

Business Email Compromise is when scammers impersonate someone you trust in business — usually through email — to trick you into sending money, sharing sensitive information, or changing payment details.

Here's how it typically works:

- A scammer monitors your email communications (sometimes for weeks)
- They learn who you do business with, how you talk, and when invoices are due
- They send a fake email that looks identical to one from your accountant, supplier, or client
- The email asks you to pay an invoice to a "new" bank account, or to urgently update payment details
- You transfer the money — and it's gone

The ACCC reported that Australian small businesses lost over $227 million to business email compromise scams in the last two years. And because the payments look legitimate, many business owners don't realise they've been scammed until it's too late.

## Why Sunshine Coast Small Businesses Are at Risk

If you're a builder coordinating with subbies, an accountant managing client payments, or a retail shop owner dealing with suppliers, you're moving money via email regularly. That makes you a target.

Imagine this: You run a landscaping business and you're halfway through a big commercial job. You get an email from your equipment supplier saying their bank details have changed and asking you to pay the next invoice to a new account. The email looks perfect — same logo, same signature, same tone. You pay the $8,000 invoice. Two weeks later, your actual supplier calls asking where the payment is.

Scammers don't need to hack into your systems with sophisticated malware. Often, they just need:

- A leaked password from an old data breach
- Access to a staff member's compromised email account
- Publicly available information from your website or social media

This is why small business cyber security isn't just about antivirus software anymore — it's about understanding how these scams work and putting simple protections in place.

## How to Protect Your Business from Email Compromise

The good news? You don't need an IT department to protect yourself. Here are practical steps any Sunshine Coast small business can take:

### Verify Payment Changes

- Always call the person directly (using a number you already have — not one in the email) to confirm any change in bank account details
- Never assume an email is legitimate just because it looks right

### Use Multi-Factor Authentication

- Turn on two-factor authentication (2FA) for your email, banking, and business apps
- This adds an extra security step that makes it much harder for scammers to access your accounts

### Check for Red Flags

- Urgent language ("Pay this immediately" or "Update your details now")
- Slight changes in email addresses (e.g., john@bizness.com.au instead of john@business.com.au)
- Requests to keep things confidential or bypass normal procedures

### Educate Your Team

- If you have staff, make sure everyone knows about BEC scams
- Create a simple policy: all payment changes must be verbally confirmed

  # Amazon Job Scam Texts Target Sunshine Coast Small Business Owners

If you've received a text message lately promising easy money working for Amazon from home, you're not alone. Scammers are flooding Australian phones with fake job offers, and small business owners across the Sunshine Coast are prime targets.

## What's Really Going On?

These scam messages typically promise "high-paying, low-effort" work with Amazon. They'll tell you that you can earn hundreds or even thousands of dollars per week doing simple tasks like rating products, posting reviews, or processing orders. The messages often look legitimate, sometimes even appearing to come from Australian phone numbers.

But here's the reality: Amazon doesn't recruit workers via text message. These are sophisticated scams designed to steal your money, personal information, or both.

## Why Small Business Owners Are Being Targeted

You might wonder why scammers would target busy tradies, accommodation hosts, or café owners. The answer is simple: you're juggling multiple responsibilities, often working from your phone between jobs, and you're exactly the type of person who might be interested in a legitimate side income stream.

Imagine you're a landscaper who's just finished a big job on a Sunshine Coast property. You're checking your phone during a smoko break when a text comes through offering $500 a week for a few hours of simple online work. It sounds appealing because you know Amazon is a massive, legitimate company. That split-second of interest is all scammers need.

## How the Scam Actually Works

Once you respond to these messages, the scammers move quickly through several stages:

**Initial Contact**
- You receive a friendly response welcoming you to the "Amazon team"
- They'll often move the conversation to WhatsApp or Telegram
- Everything feels professional with logos and official-looking templates

**The Hook**
- They'll ask you to complete a few simple tasks
- Initially, they might even send you small payments to build trust
- You'll see money appearing in an online account they've set up for you

**The Sting**
- Eventually, they'll tell you there's been a "system error" or "payment processing issue"
- To fix it and access your "earnings," you need to send them money first
- They might ask for your banking details, driver's licence, or other sensitive information

By the time you realise something's wrong, the scammers have disappeared with your money and potentially enough personal information to cause serious damage to your business accounts.

## Real Risks for Sunshine Coast SMEs

For a sole trader or micro-business, falling for this scam isn't just embarrassing—it can be financially devastating. Consider an Airbnb host who hands over their banking details. Scammers could potentially access accounts containing guest payments or bond money. An electrician might lose credentials that give scammers access to their invoicing software, putting client data at risk.

This is more than a simple financial loss. It's an SME data breach waiting to happen, with potential implications for your customers' privacy and your business reputation.

## Protecting Your Business

Small business cyber security doesn't have to be complicated. Here are practical steps you can take today:

- **Delete and block** any unsolicited job offer texts immediately
- **Never send money** to claim earnings from a job you haven't properly applied for
- **Verify directly** – if you're interested in working with a company, visit their official website
- **Use separate accounts** for business banking and keep personal finances separate
- **Enable two-factor authentication** on all important business apps

## What To Do If You've Been Caught Out

If you've already responded to one of these scams or shared information, don't panic. Quick action can minimise the damage:

- Contact your bank immediately if you've shared financial details
- Change passwords on all business accounts
- Report the scam to Scamwatch
- Consider an IT security audit to check what's been compromised

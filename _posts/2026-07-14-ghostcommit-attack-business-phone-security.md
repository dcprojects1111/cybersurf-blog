  # Ghostcommit Attack: Why Your Business Phone Isn't as Safe as You Think

If you're running a small business on the Sunshine Coast, chances are your phone is your office. You're checking invoices between jobs, updating your Airbnb calendar from the beach, or confirming tomorrow's bookings while you're still cleaning up today's salon. It's convenient, it's flexible — and according to new cyber security research, it might be more vulnerable than you realise.

A new attack method called "Ghostcommit" has security experts talking, and while the technical details might sound like science fiction, the risk to small business owners is very real.

## What Is the Ghostcommit Attack?

Researchers have discovered a clever way that cybercriminals can hide malicious instructions inside ordinary image files — the kind you might receive in an email, download from a client, or save from a text message. These aren't visibly corrupted files or obvious scams. They look completely normal: a logo, a photo, a screenshot of a booking confirmation.

But hidden in the code of that innocent-looking PNG image are instructions designed to manipulate artificial intelligence tools — the same kind of AI that's increasingly built into the apps and software small businesses use every day.

When your device or app processes that image, the hidden instructions can tell the AI to do things you never intended: extract sensitive information, change data, or pass secrets along to attackers.

## Why Sunshine Coast Small Businesses Should Care

You might be thinking, "I'm just a gardener" or "I only run a small café — why would hackers target me?" But that's exactly the mindset that puts SMEs at risk.

Consider this scenario: You're an electrician who uses a job management app on your phone. A new client emails you photos of their switchboard for a quote. You open the image on your phone, and the app — which uses AI to help you generate quotes or manage workflows — processes it automatically. Hidden instructions in that image could potentially access your client database, your banking details linked to the app, or your business email.

**Small businesses are attractive targets because:**

- You handle valuable customer data (names, addresses, payment details)
- You often lack dedicated IT security measures
- You trust the tools on your phone without questioning what's happening behind the scenes
- You're busy running your business, not thinking about cyber security on the Sunshine Coast

## Real Risks for Real Businesses

The Ghostcommit attack highlights a broader vulnerability: the tools we've come to rely on can be turned against us. This matters whether you're:

- An Airbnb host managing bookings through your phone
- A hairdresser using a cloud-based booking system
- A tradie with accounting software connected to your bank
- A retail shop owner processing payments on a tablet

An SME data breach doesn't just mean stolen data. It means lost customer trust, potential legal liability, downtime while you recover, and the very real cost of rebuilding your reputation in a tight-knit community like the Sunshine Coast.

## What You Can Do Starting Today

The good news? Awareness is half the battle, and small business cyber security doesn't have to be complicated or expensive.

**Simple steps include:**

- Think twice before opening image files from unknown senders
- Keep your phone and business apps updated (those updates often include security patches)
- Use different passwords for different accounts
- Enable two-factor authentication wherever it's offered
- Consider a basic IT security audit, even if you're just a one-person operation

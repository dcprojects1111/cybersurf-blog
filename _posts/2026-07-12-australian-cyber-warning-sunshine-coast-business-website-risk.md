  # Australian Cyber Warning: Is Your Business Website at Risk?

If you're running a small business on the Sunshine Coast and your website was built a few years ago — maybe by a mate, a freelancer, or even a web agency that's since gone quiet — you need to read this.

The Australian Cyber Security Centre has just issued a warning about a major global hacking campaign targeting small business websites. Specifically, they're going after outdated content management systems (CMS) and plugins. If that sounds like tech jargon, here's what it means in plain English: the software that runs your website might have holes in it, and criminals are actively searching for those holes right now.

## What's Actually Happening?

Hackers are using automated tools to scan thousands of Australian websites every day, looking for ones running old, unpatched software. When they find a vulnerable site, they can:

- Take complete control of your website
- Steal customer data including names, emails, and phone numbers
- Install malware that infects your visitors' devices
- Use your site to launch attacks on others (which can get you in legal trouble)
- Hold your website hostage until you pay a ransom

This isn't a future threat. It's happening right now, and the ACSC doesn't issue warnings unless the risk is serious and widespread.

## Why Small Sunshine Coast Businesses Are Prime Targets

You might think, "Who'd bother hacking my little café website?" But that's exactly why you're vulnerable.

Cybercriminals aren't necessarily after *your* specific business. They're running automated scans across entire regions, and Sunshine Coast SMEs often tick all the wrong boxes:

- Websites built on popular platforms like WordPress, Joomla, or Drupal
- Plugins and themes that haven't been updated in months (or years)
- No ongoing IT security audit or maintenance
- No one actively monitoring for suspicious activity

Imagine you're a landscape gardener with a simple site showing your work and contact form. That contact form collects names, emails, phone numbers, and property addresses. If hackers access that database, they've got a goldmine of personal information to sell or use for identity fraud. Worse, if the breach becomes public, you could be facing penalties under Australian privacy laws — and the reputational damage could be devastating in a tight-knit community like ours.

## Real Impact for Real Businesses

Let's make this concrete. Picture a local accountant whose website gets compromised. Hackers replace the legitimate bank details on the "Pay Your Invoice" page with their own. Clients transfer thousands of dollars to criminals instead of paying their bills. The accountant doesn't realise until clients start complaining about unpaid invoices. The financial loss is one thing; the loss of client trust might end the business entirely.

This is the kind of SME data breach that's becoming increasingly common, and it often starts with something as simple as an outdated WordPress plugin.

## What You Can Do Today

The good news? This threat is largely preventable. Here's what every small business owner should do:

- **Check when your website was last updated** — if you can't remember, that's a red flag
- **Contact whoever built your site** and ask about security updates and patches
- **Consider if you have a backup** of your website and customer data
- **Review what customer information** you're actually collecting and storing

For small business cyber security on the Sunshine Coast, you don't need to become a tech expert. You just need to know whether you're already exposed.

# What Small Business Owners Need to Know About the Latest ANZ Phishing Scam

If you've got an ANZ account linked to your business, you might have heard whispers about a phishing scam doing the rounds. Before you panic or ignore it entirely, let's talk about what's actually happening and whether you need to do anything about it.

Here's the short version: scammers are sending convincing-looking emails and text messages pretending to be from ANZ. They're designed to trick you into clicking a link and handing over your login details. It's not new, it's not particularly sophisticated, but it is working — because these messages are getting better at looking legitimate.

## Does This Actually Affect Sunshine Coast Small Businesses?

Yes and no.

If you bank with ANZ for your business accounts, then yes — this is directly relevant to you. Even if you don't, the tactics these scammers are using apply to just about every bank, supplier portal, and cloud service you use. So while the ANZ branding might not matter to you specifically, the threat absolutely does.

Small and medium enterprises (SMEs) are prime targets for this kind of thing. Not because you're easy pickings, but because you're busy. You're juggling invoices, customer enquiries, staff issues, and a dozen other things before lunch. A urgent-looking message from your bank can slip through when your guard is down.

### What These Scams Actually Look Like

The current batch of fake ANZ messages usually include:

- A sense of urgency ("Your account will be locked unless you verify your details")
- A link that looks almost right (think anz-security-login.com instead of anz.com.au)
- Branding that's close enough to pass a quick glance
- Language that sounds official but slightly off

Think of it like a dodgy tradie quote that uses the right letterhead but gets the ABN wrong. If you're in a rush, you might not notice.

## Two Things You Can Do This Week

Here's the good news: you don't need an IT security audit or a degree in cyber security to protect yourself from this stuff. You just need two simple habits.

**Stop and hover.** Before clicking any link in an email or text message, hover your mouse over it (or long-press on mobile). The real destination will pop up. If it doesn't say exactly anz.com.au (or whatever legitimate site you're expecting), delete it. No exceptions. This takes three seconds.

**Go direct, always.** If a message claims to be from your bank, your supplier, or anyone asking you to log in — ignore the link. Open your browser, type in the web address yourself, and log in the normal way. If there's really an issue, you'll see it there. This is the digital equivalent of looking up a plumber's number yourself instead of calling the one on a flyer shoved in your mailbox.

These aren't big, complicated changes. They're just good habits — the kind that stop an SME data breach before it starts.

## The Bigger Picture

Phishing scams like this one aren't going away. They're cheap to run, easy to scale, and they work often enough to make them worthwhile for criminals. Whether you're running a café in Mooloolaba, a physio clinic in Maroochydore, or a bookkeeping business anywhere across the Sunshine Coast, you're swimming in the same digital waters as everyone else.

The key isn't to panic. It's to be skeptical, take your time, and trust your instincts. If something feels off, it probably is.

# Telstra Cuts Off Dodgy IP Addresses: What It Means for Your Business

You may have seen the headlines this week: Telstra has started blocking internet traffic from certain overseas IP addresses linked to cybercrime. It's the kind of move that sounds dramatic — and it is, in some ways — but before you wonder whether your internet connection is about to go haywire, let's talk through what's actually happening and whether it affects your Sunshine Coast business.

## What Telstra Is Actually Doing

An IP address is essentially your device's street address on the internet. Every time you connect online, your computer, phone, or server gets one. Telstra has identified a list of overseas IP addresses that are known to be involved in cyberattacks — think phishing scams, malware distribution, and attempts to break into business systems. They've now started blocking traffic from those addresses before it even reaches Australian networks.

It's a bit like a bouncer at the door of a pub. If someone's known to cause trouble, they're not getting in. Telstra's doing the same thing at the network level, stopping dodgy traffic before it hits your inbox or website.

This is part of a broader push by Australian telcos to take more responsibility for cyber security, especially for small and medium businesses that don't have dedicated IT teams watching the gates.

## Does This Affect Your Business?

**The short answer: probably not directly.**

If you're running a legitimate business on the Sunshine Coast — whether that's a physio clinic, building company, or bookkeeping practice — this change should be invisible to you. You're not going to wake up one morning unable to send invoices or check your email. Telstra isn't blocking everyday internet use.

However, **if you do business with overseas suppliers, clients, or contractors**, and one of them happens to be using a dodgy or compromised server, you might notice emails don't arrive or files don't download. It's rare, but it's worth knowing about.

### What You Should Actually Do This Week

Even if Telstra's new policy doesn't directly touch your day-to-day operations, it's a good reminder that the internet isn't as open — or as safe — as it used to be. Here are two simple things you can do right now:

- **Check your email security settings.** Make sure spam filters are turned on and working. If you're using Outlook, Gmail, or a business hosting provider, there's usually a spam or junk folder — check it once a week to make sure real messages aren't getting caught.

- **Review who has access to your systems.** This is especially important if you've had staff leave recently or if you work with freelancers. Old login details are like keys left under the doormat — eventually, someone finds them. An IT security audit doesn't have to be expensive or complicated; even a quick review can close obvious gaps.

## The Bigger Picture

Telstra's move is part of a wider trend. Governments and telcos are waking up to the fact that small businesses are prime targets for cybercriminals — not because they're careless, but because they often don't have the resources to defend themselves the way larger companies do.

That's not a criticism. You're busy running a business. But it does mean taking a few small, sensible steps now can save a world of pain later. Think of it like locking your car or updating your insurance — boring, but worth it.

# Hackers Are Now Targeting Small Business Accountants to Break Into Client Systems

## Why Sunshine Coast small businesses need to know about this growing cyber threat

If you run a small business on the Sunshine Coast, you probably trust your accountant with some of your most sensitive information — tax files, bank details, ABN credentials, and access to your business finances. But what happens when hackers realise your accountant is the easiest way to access dozens of small businesses at once?

That's exactly what's happening right now, and it's a wake-up call for every tradie, retail shop owner, and sole trader who thinks cybersecurity is just an issue for big corporations.

## The New Target: Your Trusted Advisors

Cybercriminals have worked out something clever: instead of attacking hundreds of individual businesses one by one, they can break into a single accounting firm and instantly access client data, tax portals, banking systems, and confidential financial records for dozens or even hundreds of businesses.

Recent security reports show a sharp increase in attacks targeting small accounting practices, bookkeepers, and business advisors. Once hackers get in, they can:

- Steal your banking and ATO login credentials
- Lodge fraudulent BAS statements or tax returns
- Redirect your supplier payments into their own accounts
- Access your Xero, MYOB, or QuickBooks data
- Impersonate your accountant to request sensitive information

For a Sunshine Coast café owner or landscaping business, this kind of SME data breach can be devastating — and you might not even know it's happened until money goes missing or the ATO comes knocking.

## How Does This Actually Happen?

Most small accounting firms don't have the same cyber security defences as larger practices. They're running businesses just like you — often without dedicated IT support or regular security audits.

Hackers typically get in through:

- **Phishing emails** that look like they're from the ATO, a bank, or a software provider
- **Weak or reused passwords** across multiple platforms
- **Out-of-date software** with known security holes
- **Unsecured remote access** to client files and systems

Once inside your accountant's system, hackers can monitor emails, learn how your accountant communicates, and send you convincing requests that look completely legitimate.

## A Real-World Scenario

Imagine you're a sparkie with 30 years of experience building up your business. One day you get an email from your accountant's address asking you to verify your banking details for an upcoming payment. The email looks perfect — same signature, same tone, everything checks out.

You click the link, enter your details, and think nothing of it. Two weeks later, your business account is empty and there's a $15,000 payment to an overseas supplier you've never heard of.

This isn't a scare story. It's happening to Australian small businesses right now, and small business cyber security on the Sunshine Coast is just as vulnerable as anywhere else.

## What Can You Do About It?

You don't need to become a cybersecurity expert, but you do need to be aware and take some basic precautions:

- **Verify requests directly**: If your accountant emails asking for banking details or login credentials, call them on a number you already have saved — not one in the email
- **Use unique, strong passwords**: Especially for your accounting software, email, and banking
- **Enable two-factor authentication** wherever possible
- **Be suspicious of urgency**: Scammers create panic to make you act without thinking
- **Ask your accountant about their security**: A good accountant won't be offended — they should welcome the conversation

  # Your Business Accounts Could Be at Risk: The New Push for ID Checks Online

If you're running a small business on the Sunshine Coast, you've probably noticed more websites and apps asking you to verify your identity. Maybe you're setting up a new booking system for your salon, opening a business account with a platform, or even just trying to access certain features in apps you already use. These "age verification" and identity checks are popping up everywhere — and while they're meant to keep things safer, they're also creating new cyber security risks for Sunshine Coast small businesses like yours.

## What's Changing with Online Verification?

Governments around the world — including here in Australia — are bringing in stricter rules about proving who you are online. Social media platforms, payment systems, and business software are all being asked to verify users more carefully. That often means scanning your driver's licence, taking a selfie, or uploading other personal documents.

For a café owner setting up online ordering, a tradie creating an account with a new supplier portal, or an accountant accessing cloud software, this has become routine. But here's the catch: every time you hand over that information, you're trusting another company to keep it safe.

## The Real Threat: Where Does Your Face Actually Go?

When you upload your ID or take a verification selfie, that data has to go somewhere. Many platforms send it to their servers, store it in databases, and keep it on file — sometimes indefinitely. If that company experiences an SME data breach, or their systems aren't properly secured, your biometric data could end up in the wrong hands.

Imagine this: You're a landscaper who's just signed up for a new invoicing app that required you to verify your identity with a driver's licence photo and a selfie. Three months later, that company gets hacked. Now your licence details, your face, and potentially your business information are floating around on the dark web. Scammers can use that to impersonate you, open accounts in your name, or even target your clients.

This isn't about being paranoid — it's about being realistic. Small business cyber security isn't just about your laptop or your phone. It's also about the platforms and services you trust with your personal information.

## How New Technology Is Trying to Help

The good news? Some companies are starting to use smarter technology that keeps your biometric information on *your* device instead of sending it to their servers. These systems can confirm you're over 18 (or verify your identity) without ever seeing or storing your actual photo. It's like showing your ID at the pub, but the bouncer never takes a photocopy.

This approach — called "on-device verification" — is a big step forward for privacy. But not every platform is using it yet. Many of the apps and systems small businesses rely on still collect and store sensitive data the old-fashioned way.

## What This Means for Your Sunshine Coast Business

You don't need to become an IT expert overnight, but you *do* need to start thinking about where your personal and business information is going. Every app, platform, and service you sign up for is a potential door into your business.

Here's what you should keep in mind:

* **Check what platforms are asking for.** If a tool needs your driver's licence or a selfie, ask yourself: is this really necessary? Is there an alternative?
* **Look for security signals.** Does the company explain how they protect your data? Do they mention encryption or on-device processing?
* **Assume breaches happen.** Even the biggest companies get hacked. Don't put all your trust in one basket.
* **Stay on top of your accounts.** Unusual login attempts, password reset emails you didn't request, or strange activity can all be early warning signs.

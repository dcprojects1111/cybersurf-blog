  # Russian Hacking Group Targets Accounting Software Used by Thousands of Small Businesses

## What's happened?

A sophisticated Russian cybercrime gang has been caught targeting popular cloud-based accounting and invoicing platforms used by small businesses across Australia. Security researchers discovered the group has been using stolen credentials to access business accounts, siphoning off financial data, and in some cases, redirecting payments to fraudulent bank accounts.

The attack method is clever but not new: hackers buy lists of leaked usernames and passwords from previous data breaches, then try those same login details across hundreds of different business services. Because so many of us reuse the same password everywhere, this technique — called "credential stuffing" — works frighteningly well.

## How the attack works

Here's what these Russian hackers are doing:

- **Buying stolen login details** from previous data breaches (often from completely unrelated websites)
- **Testing those credentials** across accounting software, invoicing apps, and business banking portals
- **Gaining access** to business accounts where owners have reused passwords
- **Stealing financial data** including client information, bank details, and tax records
- **Redirecting payments** by changing bank account details in invoices or payment systems

The scary part? You might not notice anything wrong for weeks or even months.

## What this means for your business

An SME data breach isn't just about lost data — it's about lost trust, lost revenue, and potentially serious legal obligations. Under Australian privacy laws, if you hold customer information and it's compromised, you may be required to notify affected individuals and the Office of the Australian Information Commissioner.

For a sole trader or micro-business, that could mean:

- Hours or days locked out of critical business systems
- Customers not receiving invoices or having payments go astray
- Personal liability for client data that's been exposed
- Damage to your local reputation that's hard to repair
- Potential fines for privacy breaches

## How to protect yourself

The good news is you don't need to become a tech expert or hire expensive IT security audit services to take the most important steps:

**Use unique passwords for every business account**
- Never reuse the same password across multiple services
- Consider a password manager app to keep track of them all

**Turn on two-factor authentication (2FA)**
- Most accounting and invoicing platforms offer this for free
- It adds a second step to login (usually a code sent to your phone)
- This single step stops most credential stuffing attacks dead

**Check if you've already been compromised**
- Your email and passwords may already be circulating on the dark web
- Previous breaches from years ago could put you at risk today

**Review who has access to your systems**
- Remove old employee accounts and contractor access
- Check your account login history for anything suspicious

  # Cyber Criminals Are Using AI to Launch Smarter Phishing Attacks — Here's What Sunshine Coast Small Businesses Need to Know

If you're running a small business on the Sunshine Coast, there's a good chance you've received a dodgy email or two. Maybe it was poorly written, full of spelling mistakes, or just felt "off" enough that you deleted it without a second thought.

Unfortunately, those days of obvious scams are disappearing fast.

Cyber criminals are now using artificial intelligence to create phishing emails that look and sound completely legitimate — and small businesses across Australia are increasingly in their crosshairs.

## What's Changed With AI-Powered Phishing?

Traditional phishing emails were often easy to spot. The grammar was clunky, the sender's email address looked suspicious, and the whole thing felt like it was written by someone who didn't speak English as their first language.

But AI has changed the game entirely.

Now, scammers can use tools like ChatGPT and similar AI platforms to:

- Write perfectly polished emails in natural, professional English
- Mimic the tone and style of legitimate businesses you already work with
- Personalise messages using information scraped from your social media or website
- Create convincing fake invoices, payment requests, and account notifications
- Generate follow-up emails that respond intelligently to your replies

The result? Phishing emails that even tech-savvy people struggle to identify as fake.

## Why Small Businesses Are Prime Targets

You might think cyber criminals only go after big corporations, but that's not the case. In fact, small businesses are often more attractive targets because they typically have:

- Weaker cyber security protections in place
- No dedicated IT support to catch suspicious activity
- Staff (or just yourself) juggling multiple roles without time for security training
- Direct access to business bank accounts and customer payment details

Imagine you're an electrician on the Sunshine Coast who just completed a big commercial job. You receive an email that appears to be from your supplier, requesting urgent payment for materials. The email looks perfect — right logo, correct contact name, professional language. You click the payment link, enter your banking details, and suddenly your business account is compromised.

Or picture this: you're managing a couple of Airbnb properties in Noosa. An official-looking email arrives claiming there's a problem with your listing that needs immediate attention. You click through and log in to "fix" the issue — except you've just handed over your account credentials to a scammer who now controls your listings and can access your guests' personal information.

These scenarios are happening more frequently as AI makes phishing attacks harder to detect.

## What an SME Data Breach Actually Costs

When small business owners on the Sunshine Coast think about cyber security, many assume it won't happen to them. But an SME data breach isn't just inconvenient — it can be financially devastating.

Consider the real costs:

- **Lost revenue** while your systems are down or compromised
- **Customer trust** that takes years to rebuild
- **Legal obligations** if client data is exposed
- **Bank fees** and time spent recovering hacked accounts
- **Reputation damage** that spreads quickly in tight-knit Sunshine Coast communities

For a café owner, losing access to your point-of-sale system during peak weekend trade could cost thousands. For an accountant, a data breach involving client financial information could end your business entirely.

## Practical Steps to Protect Your Business

The good news is that small business cyber security doesn't require a massive budget or technical expertise. Start with these essentials:

- **Verify before you click**: If an email requests urgent payment or login details, contact the sender directly using a phone number you already have — never the one in the email
- **Enable two-factor authentication** on all business accounts (banking, email, booking systems, everything)
- **Use unique passwords** for each account — a password manager makes this simple
- **Keep software updated** on all devices you use for business
- **Train anyone who works with you** to recognise suspicious requests

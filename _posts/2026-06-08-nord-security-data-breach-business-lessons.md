# VPN Provider Nord Security Faces Data Breach — What It Means for Your Business

A major cybersecurity company has just had a very public reminder that no one is bulletproof. Nord Security — the business behind popular VPN service NordVPN — recently confirmed that customer data was accessed by an unauthorised party. If you've heard of VPNs (Virtual Private Networks) but aren't quite sure what they do, think of them like a secure tunnel for your internet traffic: they're meant to keep your online activity private and protected.

So when a company whose whole job is protecting privacy gets breached, it's worth paying attention — not to panic, but to learn.

## What Actually Happened?

According to Nord Security, a third-party contractor they worked with was compromised, and that breach led to unauthorised access to some customer information. The company says no passwords or payment details were exposed, but user emails and some account-related data were accessed.

To their credit, Nord moved quickly. They notified affected customers, forced password resets, and went public with what happened. That's exactly what you want to see: transparency, not silence.

But here's the thing — this breach didn't happen because Nord's product failed. It happened because someone else in their supply chain had weaker security. And that's a reminder every business owner on the Sunshine Coast should take seriously.

## The Real Lesson: You're Only as Secure as Your Weakest Link

If you run a small business — whether you're a tradie in Caloundra, a physio in Noosa, or an accountant in Maroochydore — you probably rely on other businesses to handle parts of your operation. Maybe it's a bookkeeper with access to your Xero account. A marketing company managing your Facebook page. A website developer who still has admin login details from three years ago.

Each of those connections is a potential weak point. Not because those people are dodgy, but because if *they* get hacked, the attacker might be able to reach *your* business through them.

This is what's called a **supply chain attack**, and it's one of the fastest-growing threats to small and medium enterprises (SMEs). You don't have to be the target — you just have to be connected to someone who is.

### What You Can Do This Week

You don't need to become a cybersecurity expert or conduct a full IT security audit to reduce your risk. Here are two simple, practical steps:

- **Review who has access to what.** Make a list of anyone outside your business with login credentials — contractors, former employees, service providers. If they don't need access anymore, revoke it. If they do, make sure they're using strong, unique passwords.

- **Ask your key suppliers about their security.** You wouldn't hire a tradie without checking their insurance. Why trust a tech provider or contractor without asking how they protect your data? A simple question — "What do you do to keep client data secure?" — can tell you a lot.

## You Don't Have to Go It Alone

Most small business owners don't have time to become cyber security experts — and they shouldn't have to. But staying aware of how breaches happen and taking a few sensible precautions can make a real difference. Think of it like locking your office at night: it's basic, it's quick, and it works.

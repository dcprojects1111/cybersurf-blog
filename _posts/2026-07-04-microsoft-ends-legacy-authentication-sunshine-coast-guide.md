  # Microsoft Deprecates Legacy Authentication: What It Means for Your Sunshine Coast Small Business

## The Change That's Already Happening

Microsoft has quietly been phasing out something called "legacy authentication" across its popular business tools — Outlook, OneDrive, SharePoint, and Teams. If those names sound familiar, it's because millions of Australian small businesses rely on them every single day.

From October 2024, Microsoft began blocking older sign-in methods that don't use modern security features. By early 2025, this change will affect nearly every business still using outdated login systems. If you're running your plumbing business from your phone, managing your salon's bookings through Outlook, or storing your landscaping quotes in OneDrive, this affects you.

Here's the thing: you might not even realise you're using legacy authentication until it stops working — or worse, until someone exploits it.

## What Is Legacy Authentication (In Plain English)?

Legacy authentication is simply an older way your apps and devices log into Microsoft services. Think of it like an old lock on your shop door that only needs one key, with no deadbolt or alarm system.

Modern authentication, by contrast, is like having multiple layers of security: the key, a security code, maybe even a camera. It supports things like multi-factor authentication (that text message code you get when logging into your bank).

The problem? Those old-style logins are incredibly easy for cybercriminals to crack.

## Why This Matters to Sunshine Coast Business Owners

Imagine you're an electrician in Maroochydore. You've used the same email setup on your phone for five years. It works fine. But because it's using legacy authentication, a hacker in another country can guess your password through automated attacks — thousands of attempts per minute — without triggering any alarms.

One day you wake up to find:

- Your email account is locked
- Invoices you sent have been intercepted and altered with fake bank details
- Your clients received spam from your address
- You've lost access to years of quotes, job records, and client communications

This isn't a Hollywood scenario. SME data breaches like this happen every single day across Australia, and small businesses on the Sunshine Coast are absolutely in the crosshairs.

## What You Need to Do Right Now

The good news? Protecting yourself doesn't require an IT degree.

**Check your current setup:**

- Are you accessing Outlook or other Microsoft apps through your phone or tablet?
- When did you last update those apps?
- Do you get prompted for a security code when you sign in, or just a password?

**Take these immediate steps:**

- Update all your Microsoft apps to the latest versions
- Enable multi-factor authentication on your Microsoft account (yes, the text message thing)
- If you use an older email program on your desktop, consider switching to the web version or updating your software

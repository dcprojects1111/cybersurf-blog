  # New 'Bigpanzi' Botnet Discovered Controlling 170,000 Smart Devices Across the Globe

## Your Business Phone or Router Might Already Be Part of a Cyber Crime Network

If you're running your Sunshine Coast business from your phone, tablet, or a basic router setup at home or in your shopfront, this story might hit closer to home than you think.

Security researchers have just uncovered a massive botnet called 'Bigpanzi' that's been quietly running on over 170,000 smart devices worldwide — and it's been operating undetected since 2015. A botnet is essentially a network of hijacked devices that cyber criminals control remotely, without the owner's knowledge or permission.

The scary part? These weren't high-security corporate systems. They were everyday devices: Android TV boxes, smartphones, tablets, and home routers. The kinds of things you probably use to run your business right now.

## What Actually Happened?

Researchers found that Bigpanzi had infected devices across multiple countries, turning them into zombie workers for criminal operations. The infected devices were used to:

* Generate fake advertising clicks to steal revenue from legitimate businesses
* Launch attacks on other websites and online services
* Steal personal and business data from the device owners
* Operate as part of a streaming media piracy network

The owners of these 170,000 devices had no idea their phones, tablets, or routers were being used for criminal activity in the background.

## How Do Devices Get Infected?

The Bigpanzi botnet spread through several methods:

* Malicious apps downloaded from unofficial app stores
* Out-of-date software and operating systems with known security holes
* Cheap devices (often Android TV boxes or routers) that came pre-infected from overseas suppliers
* Clicking dodgy links in text messages or emails

Most small business owners don't have IT security audits or professional support. You're busy running your business, not monitoring whether your router firmware is up to date.

## What Can You Do Right Now?

You don't need to become a tech expert overnight, but there are some practical steps that make a real difference:

* Only download apps from official stores (Google Play, Apple App Store)
* Update your phone, tablet, and router software when prompted — those updates often fix security holes
* Use different passwords for different business apps
* Be suspicious of texts or emails asking you to click links, even if they look legitimate
* Consider replacing very old or ultra-cheap devices, especially if they're central to your business

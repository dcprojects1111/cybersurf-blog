  # Cyber Criminals Are Exploiting AI Tools to Write More Convincing Scam Emails

## Your Business Email Might Look Real — But Is It?

If you run a small business on the Sunshine Coast, you're probably used to dodging dodgy emails. The ones with spelling mistakes, weird grammar, and stories that don't quite add up. They've been easy to spot — until now.

Cyber criminals are now using artificial intelligence tools to write scam emails that look and sound completely legitimate. These aren't the clumsy phishing attempts of the past. They're professionally written, they use correct business language, and they're targeted specifically at businesses like yours.

For a local café owner juggling supplier invoices, staff rosters, and customer bookings all from their phone, the difference between a real email from their POS system provider and a fake one is becoming impossible to spot.

## What's Changed With AI-Powered Scams?

Traditionally, scam emails were full of red flags: poor English, generic greetings like "Dear Customer," and obvious urgency tactics. You could smell them from a mile away.

But AI writing tools have changed the game entirely. Criminals are now using the same technology that helps businesses write marketing copy to craft emails that:

- Use your actual business name and industry terminology
- Reference real software or services you're likely to use
- Mirror the writing style of legitimate companies
- Contain no spelling or grammar errors
- Include convincing (but fake) branding and logos

These tools can generate hundreds of personalised emails in minutes, each one tailored to look like it came from Xero, MYOB, Australia Post, or even your local council.

## A Real-World Example: The Café Invoice Scam

Imagine you're running a busy café in Mooloolaba. You receive an email that appears to be from your coffee supplier with an "updated invoice" attached. The email uses their logo, references your recent order, and the writing is spot-on.

You click the attachment to check the figures. Within seconds, malware is installed on your phone. By the end of the day, the scammers have access to your banking app, your Square account, and your supplier login details.

This isn't a far-fetched scenario. It's happening right now to small businesses across Australia, and SME data breach incidents are climbing every quarter.

## Why Small Business Cyber Security Matters More Than Ever

You might think cyber criminals only target big companies, but that's exactly what they want you to believe. Small businesses on the Sunshine Coast are actually prime targets because:

- You handle customer payments and personal information
- You probably don't have dedicated IT support
- You're busy running your business and might not notice something's wrong straight away
- You're connected to other businesses (suppliers, clients, contractors) which makes you a gateway to bigger targets

Whether you're an electrician managing quotes on the go, an accountant handling sensitive client data, or an Airbnb host with booking system access, you're handling information that criminals want.

## How to Protect Your Business

You don't need to become a tech expert, but you do need to be more cautious. Here's what you can do:

- Never click links or download attachments from unexpected emails, even if they look legitimate
- If an email asks you to verify account details or payment information, go directly to the website (don't use links in the email) or call the company
- Enable two-factor authentication on every business account that offers it
- Keep your phone and computer updated with the latest security patches
- Have a separate device or account for business banking

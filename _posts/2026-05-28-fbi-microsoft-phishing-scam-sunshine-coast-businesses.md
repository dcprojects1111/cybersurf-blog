  # New Phishing Scam Targets Microsoft Accounts Used by Sunshine Coast Small Businesses

If you're running your business from your phone or laptop using Microsoft services like Outlook, Teams, or OneDrive, there's something you need to know about. The FBI has just issued a warning about a sophisticated new phishing scam that's specifically designed to steal access to these accounts — and it's clever enough to get past the security most people think is protecting them.

For Sunshine Coast small business owners who rely on Microsoft 365 for everything from emails to customer bookings, this threat hits close to home.

## What's Actually Happening?

Cybercriminals are using something called a "phishing kit" — essentially a ready-made toolkit that makes it easy for attackers to create convincing fake login pages. This particular kit, which authorities are tracking, is specifically built to steal Microsoft account credentials.

Here's what makes it particularly nasty: it can bypass multi-factor authentication (MFA). That's the extra security step where you get a code sent to your phone after entering your password. Many business owners have set this up thinking they're fully protected, but this new scam finds ways around it.

Once attackers get into your account, they're not just taking a quick peek. They're establishing long-term access to your:

* Outlook emails and contacts
* Teams chats and shared files
* OneDrive documents and business records
* Any other Microsoft services connected to your account

## Why This Matters for Your Sunshine Coast Business

Imagine this scenario: You're an accountant with client tax records stored in OneDrive. You click what looks like a legitimate Microsoft security alert asking you to verify your login. Within hours, someone on the other side of the world has access to years of confidential client data.

Or perhaps you're a builder managing quotes, invoices, and supplier communications through Outlook. Scammers gain access, send fake invoices to your clients from your legitimate email address, and by the time you realise what's happened, thousands of dollars have disappeared into accounts you can't trace.

These aren't Hollywood movie plots. For small business owners without IT security audits or dedicated tech support, an SME data breach like this can happen frighteningly quickly.

## What Does This Scam Look Like?

The phishing emails are designed to look identical to real Microsoft notifications. They might claim:

* Your account security needs verification
* Unusual activity has been detected
* Your storage is full and needs immediate attention
* A document has been shared with you that requires sign-in

The links in these emails take you to fake login pages that look completely legitimate. When you enter your details — including that MFA code from your phone — you're handing attackers everything they need.

## Protecting Your Business Without Getting Overwhelmed

You don't need to become a cyber security expert to protect yourself. Here are practical steps any Sunshine Coast small business owner can take today:

**Think before you click.** If you receive an email asking you to log in to Microsoft, don't click the link. Instead, open a new browser window and go directly to outlook.com or office.com yourself.

**Check the sender carefully.** Look at the actual email address, not just the display name. Scammers often use addresses that are almost right but slightly off (like "micros0ft" with a zero instead of an 'o').

**Be suspicious of urgency.** Phishing emails create panic: "Your account will be closed in 24 hours!" Legitimate companies rarely operate this way.

**Use different passwords.** If your Microsoft password is the same as your banking password, change one of them immediately.

  # Scammers Are Using Fake Ads and Consent Tricks to Break Into Business Accounts

If you're running your plumbing business, café, or Airbnb from your phone and laptop, you need to know about two sneaky new scams doing the rounds right now. They don't rely on complicated hacking — they're simply tricking busy business owners into handing over access without realising it.

## What's Happening?

Cybercriminals have shifted tactics. Instead of finding weaknesses in software, they're now using what's called "social engineering" — a fancy term for manipulating people into making mistakes. Two campaigns in particular are catching out small business owners across Australia.

The first involves fake advertisements appearing on legitimate platforms like X (formerly Twitter). These ads look official, sometimes even showing a "verified" badge, but they're designed to install malicious software on Mac computers.

The second, called ConsentFix, is even sneakier. It tricks you into giving permission for a dodgy app to access your Microsoft account — your Outlook email, OneDrive files, and potentially your entire business records.

## Why Small Businesses on the Sunshine Coast Should Care

You might think cybercriminals only target big corporations, but that's exactly what they want you to believe. 

Small businesses are increasingly in the crosshairs because:

- You're less likely to have IT security measures in place
- You run critical business functions from personal devices
- You often use the same password across multiple accounts
- You're busy and more likely to click without thinking twice

Imagine this: You're a local electrician who's just finished a big job. You see an ad for accounting software that promises to make invoicing easier. You click, download what looks like a legitimate app, and suddenly someone overseas has access to your customer database, quotes, and banking details.

Or picture this scenario: You manage a short-stay accommodation business. You receive what looks like an official Microsoft security alert asking you to approve access to protect your account. You click "yes" without reading the fine print, and within hours, scammers have locked you out of your booking calendar, guest communications, and financial records.

These aren't far-fetched scenarios. They're happening right now to businesses just like yours.

## How These Scams Actually Work

### The Fake Verified Ad Trick

Scammers are paying for legitimate advertising space and making their ads look official. When you click and download what you think is genuine software, you're actually installing malware that can:

- Steal your passwords and banking information
- Access your business files and customer data
- Monitor everything you type, including sensitive information

### The ConsentFix Account Takeover

This one targets your Microsoft business account. You receive a convincing prompt asking you to grant permissions to what looks like a legitimate service. Once you click "allow," the scammers can:

- Read and send emails on your behalf
- Access all your stored documents
- Lock you out of your own account
- Use your account to scam your customers and suppliers

## Protecting Your Sunshine Coast Business

The good news? A bit of awareness goes a long way. Here's what you can do right now:

- **Slow down before clicking** — If an ad seems too good to be true, it probably is
- **Check the source** — Only download software from official websites, not ads
- **Read permission requests carefully** — If an app asks for access to your Microsoft account, ask yourself if it genuinely needs that access
- **Use different passwords** — Don't use the same password for your banking, email, and Instagram
- **Enable two-factor authentication** — This adds an extra layer of protection even if your password is compromised

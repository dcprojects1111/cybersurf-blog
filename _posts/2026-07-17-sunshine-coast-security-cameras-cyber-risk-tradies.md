  # Why Small Businesses on the Sunshine Coast Need to Think Twice About Connected Security Cameras

If you run a tradie business, a café, or a retail shop on the Sunshine Coast, you've probably thought about installing security cameras. They're getting cheaper, smarter, and easier to set up. But a growing story out of the United States is a timely reminder that "smart" doesn't always mean "secure" — and when these devices are connected to the internet, they can become a cyber security risk rather than a solution.

## The Problem With Always-Connected Cameras

A company called Flock has been rolling out automated license plate recognition cameras across the US. They're designed to help businesses and councils monitor who's coming and going. Sounds great in theory. But now, serious concerns are emerging — not just about privacy, but about accuracy and reliability.

Here's the thing: when a security system is connected to the cloud and managed by a third party, you're trusting that company with your data, your footage, and access to your premises (digitally speaking). If that system has flaws — technical glitches, data leaks, or weak cyber security — you're exposed.

And you don't need to be running a high-tech operation for this to matter. Small business cyber security issues affect sole traders and micro-businesses just as much as big corporations, often more so, because they have less protection in place.

## What This Means for Sunshine Coast Business Owners

Let's say you're a landscaper with a yard full of equipment, or a short-stay accommodation host managing three Airbnb properties. You install a few Wi-Fi security cameras so you can check in from your phone. Smart move, right?

But if those cameras aren't set up securely — default passwords unchanged, no two-factor authentication, firmware never updated — they become an open door. Hackers can access the footage, disable the camera when they want to break in, or even use the device as a entry point into other systems you use, like your booking platform or invoicing app.

One Sunshine Coast Airbnb host we spoke to recently had their account taken over after a cyber criminal accessed their property Wi-Fi through an unsecured smart device. The hacker changed the listing details, redirected bookings, and pocketed thousands in fraudulent payments before the host even noticed.

## Red Flags to Watch For

If you've installed — or are thinking about installing — internet-connected security devices, keep these warning signs in mind:

- You've never changed the default username or password
- The camera or system doesn't offer two-factor authentication
- You can't remember the last time (or if ever) the device was updated
- The footage is stored "in the cloud" but you don't know where, or who has access
- You clicked through the setup without reading the privacy policy

These aren't just IT security audit talking points — they're real vulnerabilities that can lead to an SME data breach, often without you knowing until it's too late.

## It's Not About Fear — It's About Being Smart

This isn't about scaring you away from using technology. Connected cameras, smart locks, and cloud-based tools can be brilliant for small businesses — *if* they're set up properly.

The issue is that most sole traders on the Sunshine Coast are running their business from their phone with no IT support and no time to dig into cyber security settings. That's completely understandable. But it also makes you a target.

Cyber criminals aren't just going after banks and government departments anymore. They're targeting tradie businesses, cafés, salons, and accommodation providers — because they know small business cyber security is often an afterthought.

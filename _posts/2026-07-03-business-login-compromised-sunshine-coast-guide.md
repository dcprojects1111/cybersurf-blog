  # Is Your Business Login Already Compromised? What Sunshine Coast Small Business Owners Need to Know About Account Security

## What Is an Account Takeover?

An account takeover happens when someone else gains access to your business accounts—your booking system, invoicing app, social media pages, or payment platforms. They don't smash a window or trip an alarm. They simply log in, often using passwords that were leaked years ago from other sites.

Once they're in, they can:

- Lock you out by changing your password
- Redirect payments to their own bank account
- Cancel bookings or delete customer records
- Impersonate you to scam your clients
- Hold your data ransom until you pay

## Why Sunshine Coast Tradies and Sole Traders Are Prime Targets

You might think, "I'm just a gardener" or "I only run a small café—why would anyone target me?" That's exactly why you're attractive to cybercriminals.

Small business owners on the Sunshine Coast typically:

- Use the same password across multiple platforms
- Don't have IT support or a security system in place
- Handle everything from a mobile phone with minimal protection
- Have direct access to customer data and payment systems
- Don't regularly monitor for suspicious activity

Hackers know this. They're not after your business specifically—they're after easy targets. And without basic cyber security measures, you're leaving the door wide open.

## The Hidden Cost of an SME Data Breach

Beyond the immediate financial hit, small business cyber security breaches damage something harder to rebuild: trust.

When a hairdresser's booking system gets hacked and client phone numbers are leaked, those clients think twice about coming back. When an electrician's email is compromised and used to send dodgy invoices, their reputation takes a hit.

For sole traders and micro-businesses, your reputation is everything. One data breach can take years to recover from—if you recover at all.

## What You Can Do Today

The good news? Protecting yourself doesn't require a computer science degree or expensive IT security audit. Start with these basics:

- **Use unique passwords** for each business account (yes, every single one)
- **Turn on two-factor authentication** wherever it's offered
- **Check your accounts regularly** for unfamiliar activity
- **Update your apps and phone** when prompted—those updates often include security fixes

  # Trusting Your Team's Devices Isn't Enough: What Sunshine Coast Business Owners Need to Know

If you're running a small business on the Sunshine Coast, chances are you've handed someone access to your business systems on their personal device. Maybe it's your apprentice checking the job schedule on their phone, your part-timer accessing the booking system from home, or even your teenage kid helping update your Airbnb listings on weekends.

You trust them. Of course you do.

But here's the uncomfortable truth: trust isn't a cyber security strategy.

## The Problem With "She'll Be Right"

Most sole traders and micro-businesses operate on what I call the "trust and hope" model. You give someone your login details, they promise to be careful, and you hope nothing goes wrong. For Sunshine Coast tradies, retail shops, and service providers, this approach feels natural—we're a tight-knit community where handshake deals still mean something.

The problem? Cyber criminals don't care about trust. They're looking for the weakest link, and that's often not your systems—it's the devices accessing them.

## A Real-World Scenario: The Landscaper's Wake-Up Call

Imagine you run a landscaping business in Buderim. Your nephew helps manage your Instagram and responds to quote requests through Facebook Messenger on his phone. One day, he clicks a dodgy link, and suddenly someone in Romania has access to every conversation you've had with potential clients—including their addresses, gate codes, and when they'll be away on holiday.

Your business hasn't been "hacked" in the technical sense. But your reputation? Your client relationships? They're toast.

This isn't scare-mongering. Small business cyber security breaches like this happen daily across Australia, and Sunshine Coast businesses aren't immune.

## What Actually Works

Here's what cyber security experts recommend for small businesses—and none of it requires a computer science degree:

**Set Clear Device Rules**

* Anyone accessing your business systems must have updated devices (phone or computer)
* Set a policy that passwords can never be shared via text or Messenger
* Require PIN or fingerprint locks on all devices with business access

**Use Proper Access Controls**

* Stop sharing your master login—use separate user accounts where possible
* Most booking systems, point-of-sale apps, and accounting software let you create team member access
* When someone leaves your business, remove their access immediately

**Enable Two-Factor Authentication**

* This adds a second security step (usually a code sent to your phone) when logging in
* Available on most business apps including Xero, Square, Deputy, and Facebook Business
* Takes five minutes to set up and stops 99% of account takeovers

**Regular Check-Ins**

* Once a month, review who has access to what
* Ask: "Is this person still working with me? Do they still need this access?"
* Treat digital access like you'd treat keys to your shop

## Why Sunshine Coast Businesses Are Particularly Vulnerable

Many local businesses operate lean—that's part of our charm. The hairdresser in Mooloolaba manages bookings from her phone between clients. The Caloundra electrician uses one Gmail account for everything. The Noosa café owner lets three casuals clock in using the same iPad.

This efficiency is brilliant until it isn't. One compromised device, one reused password, one dodgy app download—and suddenly your customer database is leaked, or worse, held for ransom.

An SME data breach can cost thousands in lost business, customer notifications, and reputation damage. For a business with five staff or fewer, that can be existential.

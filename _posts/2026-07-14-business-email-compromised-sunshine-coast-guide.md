  # Has Your Business Email Already Been Compromised? What Sunshine Coast Small Business Owners Need to Know

If you're running your business from your phone—managing bookings, sending invoices, or coordinating with suppliers—your email account is probably the most valuable thing you own. It's your shopfront, your filing cabinet, and your communication hub all rolled into one.

But here's something most Sunshine Coast small business owners don't realise: your email might already be compromised, and you wouldn't even know it.

## The Hidden Threat in Your Inbox

Recent cybersecurity reports show that business email compromise is one of the fastest-growing threats facing Australian small businesses. Unlike the obvious scams we've all learned to spot, modern email attacks are sophisticated and silent.

Cybercriminals aren't always after immediate access. Sometimes they quietly monitor your emails for weeks or months, learning your business patterns, your suppliers, your clients, and when money changes hands. Then, at exactly the right moment, they strike.

## How It Happens to Businesses Like Yours

Let's say you're a builder on the Sunshine Coast. You've been emailing back and forth with a client about their renovation project. Final payment is due, and you send through your bank details as you always do.

Except this time, someone's been watching. They intercept the email, change the bank details to their own account, and forward it on. Your client pays—but the money goes straight to the scammer. By the time anyone realises what's happened, the funds are long gone.

Or picture this: you run a couple of Airbnb properties in Mooloolaba. Your email gets compromised, and suddenly the scammer has access to your booking platform, your guest communications, and your payment details. They could:

- Redirect booking payments to their own account
- Cancel legitimate bookings and demand direct payment
- Access your guests' personal information
- Lock you out of your own listing management

For a hairdresser or beauty salon owner, a compromised email could mean losing access to your booking system, exposing client phone numbers and payment details, or having someone impersonate you to your suppliers and staff.

## The Warning Signs You Might Miss

Business email compromise doesn't always announce itself with flashing lights. Watch for these subtle signs:

- Clients mentioning emails you "sent" that you don't remember
- Unusual login notifications or password reset requests
- Emails in your sent folder you didn't write
- Suppliers or clients questioning changes to your bank details
- Your email app behaving strangely or logging you out unexpectedly

The problem? Most sole traders and micro-businesses on the Sunshine Coast don't have anyone watching for these signs. There's no IT department. It's just you, running everything from your phone between jobs.

## Why Small Businesses Are Prime Targets

Cybercriminals specifically target small businesses because they know you're busy, you're probably not thinking about cyber security, and you're unlikely to have sophisticated protection in place. 

An SME data breach doesn't make the news like a big corporate hack, but it can be absolutely devastating. When you're a one-person operation or have just a handful of staff, losing access to your email—or worse, having your business accounts drained—can put you under immediately.

## What You Can Do Right Now

Small business cyber security doesn't have to be complicated or expensive. Start with these basics:

- Use strong, unique passwords for your email (not the same password you use elsewhere)
- Turn on two-factor authentication wherever it's offered
- Be suspicious of any email requesting changes to payment details—even if it looks legitimate
- Regularly check your email's login history and connected devices

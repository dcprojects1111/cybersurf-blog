## When a Trusted Employee Turns on Your Business

Here's a story that should make every small business owner think twice about their off-boarding process: a former IT employee in Iowa was just sentenced to 21 months in prison for launching a cyberattack against his old workplace — a school district where he'd worked for years. After leaving the job, he used his insider knowledge to delete accounts, disrupt daily operations, and rack up tens of thousands of dollars in damage.

This wasn't some hoodie-wearing hacker halfway across the world. It was someone who knew exactly where the keys were kept, which doors to open, and how to cover his tracks. And that's what makes insider threats one of the trickiest challenges in cyber security — especially for small businesses on the Sunshine Coast and beyond.

## Why This Matters to Your Business

You might be thinking: "I run a plumbing business, not a school district. How does this apply to me?"

Fair question. But here's the thing — every business has digital keys. Your MYOB login. Your Gmail account. Your Xero invoicing system. Your Google Business profile. Maybe even your social media pages or your customer database.

When someone leaves your business — whether they resign, retire, or part ways on bad terms — those digital keys don't automatically disappear. Unless you've taken deliberate steps to revoke access, that person can still walk right back into your systems. And if they're feeling bitter, bored, or vindictive, the damage can be significant.

### What an Insider Can Actually Do

An ex-employee with lingering access can:

- Delete or corrupt customer records
- Change passwords and lock you out of your own accounts
- Send emails pretending to be you
- Access financial data or payroll information
- Sabotage bookings, quotes, or project files

In the Iowa case, the former employee caused classroom chaos and cost the district serious money. For a small business, even a fraction of that disruption could mean lost customers, blown deadlines, and a reputational hit you can't afford.

## Two Simple Things You Can Do This Week

You don't need an IT security audit or a consultant on retainer to reduce this risk. Just two straightforward actions:

**1. Make a list of who has access to what**

Sit down with a coffee and write it out. Who can log into your accounting software? Your website? Your email? Your CRM or booking system? If someone left six months ago and you're not 100% sure their access was removed, assume it wasn't.

**2. Reset critical passwords when someone leaves**

When an employee moves on, don't just wish them well. Change the passwords on shared accounts — invoicing, banking, email, social media, the works. Yes, it's a bit tedious. But it's faster than recovering from an SME data breach or rebuilding your client list from scratch.

Think of it like changing the locks when a tenant moves out. You might trust them completely. But you still wouldn't leave the spare key under the mat.

### A Quick Sunshine Coast Reality Check

Most small businesses in our region operate on trust and lean teams. That's a strength — until it becomes a vulnerability. You don't need to become paranoid or treat staff like criminals. But a little administrative hygiene goes a long way. Especially when the cost of prevention is 10 minutes and a notebook, and the cost of a breach can be your livelihood.

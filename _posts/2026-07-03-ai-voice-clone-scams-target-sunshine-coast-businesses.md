  # Scammers Are Now Using AI Voice Clones to Target Australian Business Owners

## What's Happening Right Now

Cybercriminals have started using artificial intelligence to create incredibly convincing voice clones of real people — and they're using these fake voices to trick small business owners into handing over money or sensitive information.

Recent reports from Australian cybersecurity agencies show this isn't some distant future threat. It's happening now, right across the country, including to businesses just like yours here on the Sunshine Coast.

The scam works like this: criminals record just a few seconds of someone's voice (often from a social media video or voicemail message), feed it into an AI program, and within minutes can create a voice clone that sounds exactly like that person. They then use this cloned voice to make phone calls pretending to be your accountant, your supplier, a family member, or even one of your employees.

## How This Could Hit Your Business

Imagine you run a small landscaping business on the Sunshine Coast. You're out on a job in Noosa when you get a call that sounds exactly like your regular supplier in Brisbane. They say there's an urgent problem with your account and you need to verify your banking details immediately, or your materials order won't arrive tomorrow.

Under pressure, with a tight deadline, you provide the information. By the time you realise it wasn't actually your supplier calling, thousands of dollars have already been transferred out of your account.

**This exact scenario has already affected Australian small businesses**, and the technology is getting better and more accessible every month.

## Why Small Businesses Are Prime Targets

You might think scammers only target big corporations, but that's exactly what makes sole traders and micro-businesses so vulnerable. Here's why you're actually at higher risk:

- You don't have an IT department screening your calls
- You handle financial decisions yourself, often quickly and under pressure
- You likely have business information publicly available online (websites, social media, Google Business listings)
- Your voice might be in videos you've posted on Instagram or Facebook
- You probably haven't set up verification procedures for phone-based requests

Criminals know all this. They specifically target trades, hospitality venues, accommodation providers, and service businesses because they understand you're busy, you work fast, and you trust your regular contacts.

## Real Warning Signs to Watch For

**Be immediately suspicious if you receive a phone call that:**

- Asks you to verify banking details or make an urgent payment
- Requests passwords, PIN codes, or two-factor authentication codes
- Claims to be from someone you know but the call quality seems slightly off or "digital"
- Pressures you to act immediately without time to think
- Comes from a number you don't recognise, even if the voice sounds familiar

**What the experts recommend:**

- Hang up and call the person back on a number you already have saved
- Set up a verbal passphrase with your accountant, bookkeeper, and key suppliers
- Never provide financial information over the phone unless you initiated the call
- Be extremely cautious about posting videos where you're speaking on public social media
